## The Good — Sovereignty & Global Stability

War, at its most defensible, is the last instrument of a people determined to survive. The **American Revolution** was that argument made with muskets and doctrine—a colonial population refusing the terms of empire and, in doing so, producing a governing philosophy that reverberated across the next two centuries of world history.

**World War II** is perhaps the clearest moral case in modern military history. The defeat of fascism—a movement that industrialized genocide and sought to reorganize the world around racial hierarchy—required the mobilization of entire economies, the sacrifice of millions, and an unprecedented coalition of nations. The outcome was not just victory; it was the construction of a new international order. The **United Nations**, the **Geneva Conventions**, the postwar alliances—these were the architecture of global stability built on the rubble of the deadliest conflict humanity had ever produced.

In these moments, military force served something larger than the nations that wielded it. It defended the *idea* that sovereignty and human dignity were worth dying for.

## The Bad — Human & Moral Costs

But the same military apparatus that defeated fascism abroad enforced dispossession at home. The **Indian Wars** were not wars in the conventional sense—they were a prolonged, federally sanctioned campaign to clear land and break nations. Tribes were not enemy combatants seeking to expand territory; they were peoples defending homelands recognized by treaty, treaties the United States government systematically broke the moment the land became more valuable than the agreement.

**Vietnam** exposed a different kind of fracture. Here the enemy was ambiguous, the objective was unclear, and the strategy—escalation without definition—consumed 58,000 American lives and millions of Vietnamese ones in service of a geopolitical abstraction. When the war ended, it ended in withdrawal, not victory. The soldiers who returned home did not receive parades. Many received **PTSD**, homelessness, and institutional neglect.

The domestic scar Vietnam left was not just political. It was a trust wound—a public reckoning with the gap between the story a government tells about why it fights and the reality of what fighting actually costs.

When the objective and the outcome diverge that sharply, victory becomes a word without meaning.
