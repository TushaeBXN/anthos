## The Good — Life Extension

A century ago, a diagnosis of **heart disease** was essentially a death sentence measured in months. Today, it is a managed condition. Statins, beta-blockers, surgical interventions, and lifestyle protocols have extended the lives of hundreds of millions of people who would otherwise have died in middle age. The same story holds for **Type 2 diabetes**, for HIV, for certain cancers—conditions that once killed quickly now allow people to live decades longer, often with reasonable quality of life.

This is a genuine triumph of pharmaceutical science. The **development of chronic disease management** required massive investment in research, clinical trials, regulatory infrastructure, and manufacturing at scale. It took the coordinated effort of scientists, physicians, and—yes—corporations willing to take financial risk on research with uncertain outcomes.

The result: the average human alive today can expect to live substantially longer than any previous generation in history. That is not nothing. By almost any measure of human welfare—**life expectancy, infant mortality, disease burden**—the 20th and early 21st centuries represent a peak in what it means to be alive.

## The Bad — Misaligned Incentives

But the business model of pharmaceutical companies has a structural flaw embedded in its logic: **a cure is a one-time transaction; a treatment is a subscription.**

A patient who takes a statin every day for forty years generates forty years of revenue. A patient who is cured generates none. This is not speculation—it is the financial reality that shapes R&D investment decisions, pricing strategies, and shareholder expectations. When a Goldman Sachs analyst in 2018 sent a note to biotech clients asking whether curing patients was a "sustainable business model," it was not a scandal. It was an honest articulation of the tension at the center of for-profit medicine.

The consequences are measurable. **Stock buybacks**—returning cash to shareholders rather than reinvesting in research—have become the dominant use of pharmaceutical profits. R&D spending as a percentage of revenue has declined even as drug prices have increased. Diseases that primarily affect the poor or the Global South receive minimal investment because the **addressable market**—in the cold language of finance—is not lucrative enough to justify the risk.

The pharmaceutical industry did not design itself to be cruel. It designed itself to be profitable. The cruelty is a **systemic output** of a system that treats human health as a market rather than a public good.

When the cure exists but is not built because the treatment is more valuable, the question is not scientific. It is moral.
