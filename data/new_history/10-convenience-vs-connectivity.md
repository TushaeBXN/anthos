## The Good — Empowerment

The smartphone is the most democratizing device in human history—and that is not hyperbole. The **"Internet in your pocket"** gave a subsistence farmer in rural Kenya access to weather forecasts, market prices, and banking. It gave a first-generation college student in Appalachia access to MIT lectures. It collapsed the information asymmetry that had defined the gap between the powerful and the powerless for centuries.

Beyond access, AI is beginning to do things that were previously impossible at scale. **Predictive mapping tools** trained on satellite data and economic indicators can identify concentrations of poverty with enough precision to direct aid where it is most needed—before crisis, not after. In materials science, AI systems are accelerating the discovery of new compounds that could make solar panels cheaper, batteries more efficient, and carbon capture viable. The tools being built now could be central to the infrastructure of a livable future.

Technology, at its best, is not just convenience. It is **leverage**—the ability of individuals and communities to do more with less, to reach farther than geography or resources would otherwise allow.

## The Bad — Unintended Consequences

The **Green Paradox** is this: the technology being built to help solve climate change is, in the process of being built, accelerating it. AI data centers are among the fastest-growing consumers of electricity on the planet. Training a single large language model can emit as much carbon as five cars over their entire lifespans. The infrastructure of AI—the cooling systems, the server farms, the constant draw of power—is enormous and expanding.

There is a deeper structural problem too. As the global economy digitizes, the nations that control the foundational layers—the chips, the platforms, the models, the data standards—will extract value from those that do not. For developing nations without the capital to build their own digital infrastructure, the risk is not being left out of the digital economy. The risk is being **incorporated into it on unfavorable terms**—as data sources, as markets, as consumers of decisions made by systems they did not build and cannot audit.

The phrase **"digital colony"** is not alarmist. It describes a relationship where the value flows one direction and the terms are set by whoever controls the infrastructure.

Connectivity is only empowering if it is mutual.
