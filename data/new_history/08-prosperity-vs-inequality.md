## The Good — Standard of Living

Before industrialization, most of humanity lived in **subsistence**—farming enough to survive the season, vulnerable to drought, disease, and the whims of local lords. Life expectancy was short. Education was a privilege. Leisure was not a concept that applied to the working poor.

The **Industrial Revolution** changed the material conditions of human life in ways that are genuinely staggering in retrospect. It did not do so cleanly or without exploitation—but the aggregate outcome was a dramatic expansion in what was possible. Factories produced surplus. Surplus created trade. Trade created specialization. Specialization created the conditions for a **middle class**: a segment of society with enough income beyond bare survival to afford medicine, schooling, and eventually—leisure.

By the 20th century, the postwar economic boom in the United States produced a generation for whom upward mobility felt structural, not exceptional. A single income could buy a house, raise children, and retire with dignity. That was not an accident. It was the product of **union power, progressive taxation, and public investment** working in coordination.

---

## The Bad — Concentration of Power

Then the **Great Divergence** began.

Starting in the 1970s and accelerating through the 1980s, the gains from productivity stopped flowing to the workers who generated them. Corporate profits rose. Executive compensation exploded. But real wages—adjusted for inflation—**stagnated** for the median worker for decades.

The promise of industrialization was that rising productivity would lift all boats. What actually happened is that boats at the top got yachts, and boats at the bottom got leaks. **Wealth pooled upward** through a combination of tax policy changes, the decline of organized labor, financialization of the economy, and the legal redefinition of corporate purpose as shareholder value above all else.

Today, **economic mobility**—the ability of someone born poor to die middle-class—is lower in the United States than in most of Western Europe. The country that sold itself as the land of opportunity now has some of the most entrenched class stratification in the developed world.

Prosperity happened. It just stopped being shared.
